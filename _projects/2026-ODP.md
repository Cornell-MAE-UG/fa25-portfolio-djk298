<h3>Client &amp; Stakeholders</h3>

<p>
  <strong>Cornell CALS Extension</strong> &nbsp;·&nbsp;
  <strong>E&amp;J Gallo Winery</strong> &nbsp;·&nbsp;
  <strong>National Grape</strong>
</p>

<h3>The Problem</h3>

<p>
  Grape farmers in the Northeast US are threatened by Spotted Lanternflies (SLFs) that infiltrate
  harvests. The SLF population grows rapidly — many nymphs survive to adulthood, where they
  reproduce abundantly, increasing their population by a factor of <strong>5.47</strong>. Current
  solutions primarily target adults, but by that stage rapid population growth has already occurred,
  increasing severity regardless of intervention.
</p>
<p>
  Decreasing the SLF reproduction factor below 5.47 would slow population increase and potentially
  stagnate it in a given area, reducing the number of SLFs per vine from the current level of
  approximately 400.
</p>

<h3>Proposed Solution 1 — The Scraper</h3>

<p>
  The Scraper is a device that makes it quicker and simpler to remove SLF egg masses and improves
  accessibility in hard-to-reach places. The user spots egg masses visually (or using an integrated
  light feature), extends the scraper via a telescoping mechanism, and removes the egg masses from
  the surface into the scraper housing.
</p>

<p><em>Why it's better than the status quo:</em></p>
<ul>
  <li>Current SLF scrapers are credit-card-sized and require close contact with egg masses</li>
  <li>Our scraper makes the process simpler and improves overall accessibility</li>
</ul>

<p>
  For our MVP, we will create a prototype that extends and contains egg masses along with a
  preliminary UI. Long-term goals include a working camera system and reproducible manufacturing.
</p>

<h3>Proposed Solution 2 — Nymph Netting</h3>

<p>
  Our second solution involves weaving netting in a spiral formation to funnel and trap nymph SLFs
  that are climbing trees to feed (nymphs cannot fly). This specifically targets the nymph stage,
  preventing them from reaching adulthood. The design incorporates recent research showing that SLF
  nymphs are attracted to high-frequency vibrations, which can be used to draw them into the trap.
</p>

<h3>Key Risks &amp; Unknowns</h3>
<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Ecological impact of netting:</strong> The netting may unintentionally affect other
    wildlife. We plan to mitigate this by testing smaller-scale traps with different entry funnel
    configurations.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>User adoption of the scraper:</strong> Even an effective scraper will have no impact
    without consistent use. We will conduct user surveys to prioritize feedback and iterate on
    the design accordingly.
  </li>
</ul>

<h3>Questions for the Client</h3>

<ol>
  <li>
    <strong>What are your biggest gripes with current scrapers used to remove egg masses?</strong><br>
    <em>Decision affected:</em> This will enable us to improve user comfort and efficiency — we can
    modify the scraper shape or material to better handle specific textures or geometries.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>What traps are currently in use — how do they lure SLFs, and are they effective?</strong><br>
    <em>Decision affected:</em> We can learn from existing approaches to avoid ideas that don't work
    in practice and refine our netting design accordingly.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>What are the main barriers to removing egg masses: height, surface geometry, or consistency?</strong><br>
    <em>Decision affected:</em> Knowing this will let us design the scraper geometry for as many
    situations as possible and clarify which features to prioritize.
  </li>
</ol>

<h3>Purpose of the Prototype</h3>

<p>
  The functional prototype was built to test our core mechanical systems — the springs allowing for
  deflection of the scraper blade, the spring hinge that allows the scraper to change angles, and an
  ability to move the blade to reach sharp corners and crevices. The goal was not a finished product,
  but a working proof-of-concept that could generate real data to inform the next design iteration.
</p>

<h3>What Was Tested</h3>

<p>We ran three design tests:</p>

<p>
  <strong>Test 1 — Blade Angle Conformity.</strong> The angle of the scraper blade must vary to conform to different surfaces. Ideally, it can vary to a radius of curvature of 4”. This tests the scraper blade itself, springs, and spring hinge mechanism. To conduct this test, we will measure the displacement of the tip of the scraper blade from its initial position to its fully ‘bent’ position. This will allow us to calculate the angle and determine how much the ends of the blade are able to conform. 
</p>

<p>
  <strong>Test 2 — User Comfort &amp; Force Transfer.</strong> The force a user must apply to the
  scraper blade should fall within a comfortable range. We had 10 participants scrape modeled egg
  masses off both flat and curved surfaces, then rate their comfort on a scale of 1–10. This tests the scraper blade and the length/general sizing of the handle. This will be especially useful as we move towards modeling and understanding the telescoping handle in the future. To test this, we will have 10 people try to scrape off modeled egg masses. Each participant will rank the ease and comfort of the force they are applying so that the handle can be modeled for the average person’s strength.
</p>

<p>
  <strong>Test 3 — Cyclic Durability.</strong> Through cyclic use, the scraper blade must not experience significant deformation beyond ¼”. This tests the scraper blade and the spring mechanisms. This ensures that with repeated use, the mechanism does not degrade and does not apply a force on the blade such that it enters the plastic deformation region. To test this, we will take measurements and record the scraper blade and spring displacement before any scraping. Then, we will scrape off modeled egg masses 40 times and retake these measurements to see if the scraper blade has deformed and if the springs have changed. We will also visually inspect the blade and mechanism for any visible damage. 
</p>

<h3>Results &amp; Outcomes</h3>

<div class="callout">
  <strong>Test 1 — Blade Angle Conformity:</strong> We measured that the center of the tip of the scraper can deflect at most about 0.25” when pressed against a smooth round surface. Using that measurement and the fact that the two edges of the PVC blade are 4 ¼” apart, we can calculate that the minimum radius of curvature our scraper can wrap around is about 9.16”. This is constrained by the mounting and flexibility of our blade. From this test, we can conclude that the placement of the springs/bolts and the flexibility of the blade are insufficient, as they don’t allow the blade to wrap around surfaces with our benchmark radius of curvature. We will likely keep a similar design and dimensions, but change the thickness of the scraper blade to allow for more flexibility in the blade itself.
</div>

<div class="callout">
  <strong>Test 2 — User Comfort &amp; Force Transfer:</strong> On flat surfaces, all 10 users rated
  the experience as comfortable. On curved surfaces, however, 9 out of 10 found it required
  uncomfortable force to bend the 1/16" acrylic blade against the surface before scraping. Users
  preferred making multiple flat passes over a single conforming pass. This points to the blade
  being too stiff — the next prototype will use a thinner ~1/32" blade and a larger handle to reduce
  required force.
</div>

<div class="callout">
  <strong>Test 3 — Cyclic Durability:</strong> After 40 scraping cycles, no meaningful deformation
  was observed. The blade and springs returned to their original positions, with deflection under
  1/32" — well within the ¼" threshold. The materials proved durable enough to carry forward into
  future prototypes, though we will re-run this test after changing the blade thickness.
</div>

<h3>Next Steps</h3>

<p>
  Based on these results, our priorities for the next prototype are:
</p>
<ul>
  <li>Reduce blade thickness from 1/16" to ~1/32" acrylic to improve flexibility on curved surfaces</li>
  <li>Scale up the handle size and work toward a telescoping handle design to reduce user effort</li>
  <li>Re-run durability testing with the thinner blade to confirm it holds up over repeated use</li>
  <li>Test on a wider variety of outdoor surfaces (tree bark, fence posts, etc.) for more realistic data</li>
</ul>
